Japanese beetle control using a native pathogen (2022 - present)

Japanese beetles are a major economic pest, and the most effective control currenly is conventional insecticides. Previous efforts to introduce parasitoids from Japanese beetles' native range were ineffective. In 1987, Andreadis and Hanula described a pathogen (Ovavesicula popillae) that is native the the United States and infects scarab beetles. It was shown by Smitley et al. 2011 to increase overwintering mortality of larvae and reduce the fecundity of adult females. There has been a 75% reduction of Japanese beetles in Michigan, which is attributed to Ovavesicula popillae.

In our research, we are sampling areas with turf that would be good habitat for Japanese beetles to determine whether Ovavesicula popillae is present in Minnesota. Once we have found evidence of the pathogen, we will spread spores to infect a greater area of the state. This will lead to long-term, lasting control of Japanese beetle and potentially limit their ability to continue spreading westward.

Honey bee parasites and toxicology

Honey bees are an important pollinator and an interest for hobbyists. Due to the commercialization of honey bees and their establishment in the US, they have many pests and parasites. The most detrimental of which is Varroa destructor. Varroa are a mite that consume the fat body of bees, vector viruses, and ultimately lead to the downfall of colonies. Since they have been a pest for a long time, they have become resistant to many miticides used for their control. As such, new compounds must be tested for control.

Another important parasite is Nosema, which is a microsporidian (fungal) parasite. Spores of this parasite are spread through water sources and brought back to the hive. Although treatments do exist, they are ineffective when compared to the natural phenology of the parasite. I performed data analysis on four experiments that tested different products aimed at beekeepers. You can view the publication here.
